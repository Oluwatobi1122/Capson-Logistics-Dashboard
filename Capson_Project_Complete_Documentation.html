Section 01

How the Analysis Was Done

This is the plain English version of everything I did — from the raw Excel files all the way to the finished Power BI report. No jargon, just the real steps.

Step 1 — Joining all 14 files in Excel

The dataset came as 14 separate Excel files. Each file was its own table — Drivers, Trucks, Loads, Trips, Routes, Customers, Fuel Purchases, Maintenance Records, Safety Incidents, Delivery Events, Facilities, Trailers, Driver Monthly Metrics, and Truck Utilisation Metrics.

Before I even opened Power BI, I had to bring all of these into one workbook. I used Excel to do that. Each sheet kept its own name so I could track which data came from where. The most important thing I checked was that every table had a matching key — for example, load_id in Loads matched load_id in Trips. Without that, nothing connects properly in Power BI.

💡
The biggest lesson from this step — clean data before you build anything. A dashboard built on messy data will always give you wrong answers, no matter how good your DAX is.

Step 2 — Loading into Power BI

Once the Excel workbook was clean, I loaded it into Power BI using Get Data → Excel Workbook. I selected all 14 sheets at once. Power BI brought them in as separate tables in the data model.

After loading, I went to Model view and set up the relationships between tables. The key relationships were:

Step 3 — Writing DAX measures

Instead of dragging raw columns into visuals, I created DAX measures for every single number on the dashboards. This is the right way to do it because measures respect the relationships in your model and respond correctly to slicers and filters.

I wrote 25 measures in total across the 4 dashboards — covering revenue, OTD rates, MPG, utilisation, fuel costs, maintenance costs, safety claims, and profit.

Section 02 · Dashboard 1

DAX Measures — Revenue Overview

These are the 6 measures I created for the Revenue dashboard. Every number you see on that page comes from one of these.

Total Revenue KPI Card

Adds up every revenue value across all 85,410 loads. This is the headline number — $262.5M over 3 years.

Total Revenue = SUM( Loads[revenue] )

Avg Monthly Revenue KPI Card

Divides total revenue by the number of distinct months. Gives us the $7.29M monthly average.

Avg Monthly Revenue = DIVIDE( SUM( Loads[revenue] ), DISTINCTCOUNT( Loads[load_date].[Month] ) )

Total Loads KPI Card

Counts every row in the Loads table. Each row is one shipment.

Total Loads = COUNTROWS( Loads )

Active Customers KPI Card

Counts only customers whose account_status is Active. Filters out inactive accounts so the number reflects who is actually trading with us.

Active Customers = CALCULATE( COUNTROWS( customers ), customers[account_status] = "Active" )

Revenue by Month Line Chart

Used on the monthly trend line chart. Place load_date (Month level) on the X-axis and this measure on the Y-axis. Add Year to the Legend to get the 3 coloured lines.

Revenue by Month = CALCULATE( SUM( Loads[revenue] ), ALLEXCEPT( Loads, Loads[load_date] ) )

Revenue by Booking Type Donut Chart

Powers the donut chart. Drag booking_type to the Legend field and this measure to Values. Power BI slices the donut automatically — Dedicated 49.6%, Spot 25.3%, Contract 25.1%.

Revenue by Booking Type = CALCULATE( SUM( Loads[revenue] ), ALLEXCEPT( Loads, Loads[booking_type] ) )
Section 02 · Dashboard 2

DAX Measures — Driver Performance

These 6 measures drive every visual on the driver dashboard. The most important one is Driver Avg OTD Rate — if you drag the raw column instead of using this measure, your rankings will be wrong.

Fleet Avg OTD Rate KPI Card

The fleet-wide average on-time delivery rate. Came out at 44.6% — meaning less than half of deliveries arrived on time.

Fleet Avg OTD Rate = AVERAGE( 'Driver Montly Metrics'[on_time_delivery_rate] )

Fleet Avg MPG KPI Card

Fleet-wide average miles per gallon. Held at 6.50 across all 36 months — impressively consistent.

Fleet Avg MPG = AVERAGE( 'Driver Montly Metrics'[average_mpg] )

Avg Monthly Revenue per Driver KPI Card

Average revenue each driver earns per month — came out at $57,600.

Avg Monthly Revenue per Driver = AVERAGE( 'Driver Montly Metrics'[total_revenue] )

Driver Avg OTD Rate Bar Chart

This is the measure that powers the Top 10 drivers ranking. Use AVERAGEX — not a simple AVERAGE — so each driver gets their own correct average instead of being blended together.

Driver Avg OTD Rate = AVERAGEX( VALUES( 'Driver Montly Metrics'[driver_id] ), CALCULATE( AVERAGE( 'Driver Montly Metrics'[on_time_delivery_rate] ) ) )

Driver Total Revenue Horizontal Bar

Total revenue per driver summed across all months. Used in the Top 10 Revenue bar chart.

Driver Total Revenue = CALCULATE( SUM( 'Driver Montly Metrics'[total_revenue] ), ALLEXCEPT( 'Driver Montly Metrics', 'Driver Montly Metrics'[driver_id] ) )

Driver Count Distribution Chart

Counts distinct drivers per OTD bucket. Used in the OTD distribution column chart.

Driver Count = DISTINCTCOUNT( 'Driver Montly Metrics'[driver_id] )

Section 02 · Profit Fix

Profit DAX — Correct Formula

If your profit is showing $231M instead of $158.5M, it means the Purchases table is not being picked up by your measure. Here is why that happens and how to fix it.

❌ What gives you $231M (wrong)
Fuel cost not included

The Purchases table has no direct relationship to Loads. If your measure just subtracts maintenance and safety claims, Power BI ignores the $95.6M fuel cost entirely — leaving you with an inflated profit number.

✅ Correct approach
Wrap each table in CALCULATE separately

Using CALCULATE independently for each cost table forces Power BI to evaluate them on their own — even if the relationships are not perfect. This pulls in the full $95.6M fuel spend correctly.

Correct Profit Measure

Profit = CALCULATE( SUM( Loads[revenue] ) ) - CALCULATE( SUM( Purchases[total_cost] ) ) - CALCULATE( SUM( 'Maintenance records'[total_cost] ) ) - CALCULATE( SUM( 'Safety incidents'[claim_amount] ) ) -- Expected result: $158,549,063 (Profit Margin: 60.4%)

Section 04

Driver Chart Fix

Two charts on the driver dashboard gave wrong results when first built. Here is exactly what went wrong and how to fix it.

Fix 1 — Top 10 OTD ranking is in the wrong order

❌ Why it happened
Power BI defaulted to SUM

When you drag the raw on_time_delivery_rate column into the X-axis, Power BI defaults to SUM — it adds up all 36 monthly values per driver instead of averaging them. Drivers with more months of data appear ranked higher. The rankings look completely different from what the actual averages show.

✅ The fix
Use the DAX measure with AVERAGEX

Create the Driver Avg OTD Rate measure using AVERAGEX and drag the measure — not the column — into the X-axis. The correct top driver is Jessica Johnson at 50.8%, not whoever Power BI was showing before.

Fix 2 — OTD distribution chart has wrong buckets

❌ Why it happened
Buckets included empty ranges

The original bucket formula included ranges below 36% and above 52%. No driver in the dataset actually falls in those ranges — so those bars showed zero, making the chart look broken and unbalanced.

✅ The fix
Use only 4 buckets that match the real data

All 150 drivers fall between 36.5% and 50.8%. Use exactly 4 buckets: 36–40%, 40–44%, 44–48%, 48–52%. The chart will now show a proper bell-shaped distribution with 44–48% as the tallest bar.

Corrected OTD Bucket calculated column

OTD Bucket = VAR avgOTD = CALCULATE( AVERAGE( 'Driver Montly Metrics'[on_time_delivery_rate] ), ALLEXCEPT( 'Driver Montly Metrics', 'Driver Montly Metrics'[driver_id] ) ) RETURN SWITCH( TRUE(), avgOTD < 0.40, "1. 36-40%", avgOTD < 0.44, "2. 40-44%", avgOTD < 0.48, "3. 44-48%", "4. 48-52%" ) -- Numbers at the front (1. 2. 3. 4.) force the correct sort order -- Without them, Power BI sorts alphabetically and puts 40-44% before 36-40%

Revenue

The company made $262.5 million over 3 years. Every single quarter landed between $21.5M and $22.2M. That kind of consistency does not happen by accident — it means the business has a reliable customer base and stable demand. Dedicated bookings drive 49.6% of all revenue, which is the right kind of income — predictable and recurring.

Driver Performance

The fleet OTD rate of 44.6% is the most important finding in the whole dataset. Less than half of deliveries arrived on time — and this did not improve at all across three years. When you look at the distribution, 73 out of 150 drivers all cluster in the same 44–48% band. That tells you this is not a driver problem. You cannot coach 73 people into better performance when the same constraint is holding all of them back. The problem is the system — the scheduling, the routes, or the customer time windows.

Fleet & Maintenance

The trucks ran at 83% utilisation on average — solid, but it means 17% of fleet capacity sits idle. That is roughly 20 trucks worth of capacity available to take on more loads without buying a single new vehicle. Fleet MPG held at exactly 6.50 across all 36 months. That level of consistency across 120 trucks over 3 years is genuinely impressive — it points to good maintenance discipline and a standardised fleet.

Safety & Fuel

Fuel spend dropped by $4.3 million from 2022 to 2024. That sounds great until you realise none of it came from the fleet getting more efficient — it was entirely because the price of fuel fell. The MPG did not move. That means the moment fuel prices go back up, that saving disappears. Building actual driver efficiency habits would create savings that survive a price rise.

170 safety incidents generated $2.65M in claims. The hard number is this: 64 of those incidents — 37.6% — were classified as preventable. That is roughly $997,000 in claims that did not have to happen. Equipment Damage was the biggest single cost at $741K across 35 incidents.

4 Recommendations

1. Fix the OTD rate

Review route scheduling and customer time-window commitments. The goal should be 50%+ fleet average. This would directly reduce Customer Complaint claims and strengthen contract renewals.

2. Cut preventable incidents

Run targeted coaching on Equipment Damage handling and DOT compliance. These two categories alone account for $1.32M in claims. Even cutting preventable incidents by half saves almost $500K per year.

3. Fill the idle capacity

17% of fleet time is unused. That is 20 trucks sitting doing nothing while fixed costs keep running. A sales push to grow Dedicated or Contract bookings would improve revenue without adding any new costs.

4. Build real fuel efficiency habits

The $4.3M fuel saving is all from cheaper pump prices — not from drivers or operations doing anything differently. Idle reduction programmes and speed compliance training would lock in savings regardless of what the market does next.
